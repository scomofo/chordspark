# Stickiness Layer: 9 Highest-Retention ADHD Techniques

## Applied to ChordSpark & PianoSpark

This document identifies the 9 techniques with the strongest evidence for long-term behavioral persistence in ADHD populations, drawn from peer-reviewed research, and specifies exactly how each one maps to the music learning curricula.

---

## 1. STRETCHING THE RATIOS (Reinforcement Thinning)

**What it is:** Start with continuous reinforcement (reward every correct action), then gradually reduce reward frequency to a variable schedule. This is the single most effective technique for building behavioral persistence in ADHD — it overcomes the well-documented partial reinforcement deficit while building extinction-resistant habits.

**The science:** Luman et al. (2019, Research on Child and Adolescent Psychopathology) showed that "stretching the ratios" — gradually weaning reinforcement density from 100% to ~20% — produced acquisition speed comparable to continuous reinforcement AND behavioral persistence comparable to partial reinforcement in ADHD children. This is the best of both worlds: fast learning + durable habits.

**How to implement:**

Phase 1 (Sessions 1–5): **Continuous reinforcement.** Every clean chord gets a sound effect, XP pop-up, and visual celebration. Every transition earns points. The app is a dopamine fountain. This builds the initial behavior quickly.

Phase 2 (Sessions 6–14): **Rich variable ratio (VR-2 to VR-3).** Rewards appear after roughly every 2nd or 3rd correct action, but unpredictably. Sometimes after 1 action, sometimes after 4. The learner never knows when the next reward hits. This introduces the "slot machine" engagement pattern — the most extinction-resistant reinforcement schedule known to behavioral science.

Phase 3 (Sessions 15–30): **Lean variable ratio (VR-5 to VR-8).** Rewards are rarer but bigger when they hit. "Jackpot" moments: a song completion might randomly trigger a bonus XP burst, a new badge, or a surprise song unlock. Between jackpots, the learner maintains high response rates because the next reward could be on the very next action.

Phase 4 (Sessions 30+): **The behavior is self-sustaining.** Intrinsic reward (the sound of music, the satisfaction of playing) has replaced extrinsic reward. External rewards become rare surprises, not expectations.

**Critical rule:** NEVER go from continuous to sparse reinforcement in a single jump. That produces the ADHD "cliff" — immediate disengagement. The gradient must be smooth.

## 2. IMPLEMENTATION INTENTIONS (If-Then Planning)

**What it is:** Pre-committing to a specific if-then plan that links a situational cue to a desired action. "If [situation X], then I will [action Y]."

**The science:** Gawrilow & Gollwitzer (2008) demonstrated that implementation intentions improved response inhibition in ADHD children to the level of neurotypical controls. Paul et al. (2007) confirmed this with ERP data — if-then plans normalized the P300 brain response in ADHD children. A meta-analysis by Toli et al. (2016) confirmed effectiveness across clinical populations. Research shows implementation intentions **double or triple** follow-through rates. For ADHD specifically, they create "strategic automaticity" — the deliberate plan triggers automatic behavior, bypassing the executive function deficits that normally block action initiation.

**How to implement:**

**Session-start ritual:** Each session opens with a visible if-then prompt the learner reads (or hears):

For guitar:
- "If I sit down with my guitar, then I will play my warm-up chord (Em) three times."
- "If I hear the Spark clip, then I will place my fingers on the first chord."
- "If my fingers miss a chord change, then I will slow down and try again (not stop)."

For piano:
- "If I open the piano lid, then I will play C-E-G three times."
- "If I hear the Spark clip, then I will place my right hand in C position."
- "If both hands aren't syncing, then I will play right hand alone first."

**Practice-trigger intention:** During onboarding, the app prompts: "Complete this sentence: 'Every day, when I [specific daily event], I will open ChordSpark/PianoSpark.'" Examples:
- "When I finish dinner, I will open ChordSpark."
- "When my morning coffee is brewing, I will do my piano session."
- "When I sit down after work, I will pick up my guitar."

The app stores this intention and uses it in push notifications: "Coffee's on? Time for your chord session!"

**Error-recovery intentions:** These are critical for ADHD. Pre-plan what to do when things go wrong:
- "If I miss a day, then I will do a 5-minute session the next day (not a full one)."
- "If I feel frustrated, then I will drop back to an easier chord pair for 60 seconds."
- "If I've been away for a week, then I will tap 'Welcome Back' and do the refresher."

## 3. OBSERVATIONAL LEARNING BEFORE FEEDBACK-BASED LEARNING

**What it is:** Show the learner the correct action FIRST (through observation/demonstration), THEN have them practice with feedback. Never throw them into trial-and-error cold.

**The science:** This is a direct counter-measure to the core ADHD procedural learning deficit. Research by Lum et al. (2017, ScienceDirect) and the Nature Scientific Reports study by Frank et al. (2018) demonstrated that feedback-based procedural learning (learning by trial and error with corrective feedback) is **selectively impaired** in ADHD due to reduced striatal dopamine. However, the declarative/observational learning system (hippocampus-based) remains **intact** and may even be enhanced as a compensatory mechanism.

This means: ADHD learners struggle to learn "figure it out by trying and failing" but can learn very effectively by "watch this, then copy it."

**How to implement:**

**Every New Move segment follows this sequence:**
1. **WATCH** (30 sec): Animated demonstration of the chord shape or transition. Finger placement shown. Sound plays. Learner observes only — hands off the instrument.
2. **SHADOW** (30 sec): Learner mirrors the demonstrated action in slow motion. No feedback yet. Just copying.
3. **TRY** (2 min): Learner practices independently with gentle corrective feedback.
4. **REFINE** (1 min): Specific feedback on what to adjust.

This Watch → Shadow → Try → Refine sequence respects the intact observational learning system while gradually engaging the impaired feedback-based system. The ratio is ~30% observation, ~70% practice — not the typical 0% observation, 100% trial-and-error that most apps use.

**Song Slice segments:** Always start with 15 seconds of the song playing WITH chord highlights visible (observational priming) before the learner plays along.

## 4. PREDICTION ERROR OPTIMIZATION (Surprise-Calibrated Rewards)

**What it is:** Rewards are most effective when they violate expectations — not when they're bigger, but when they're surprising. The dopamine system doesn't code reward magnitude; it codes reward prediction error (RPE) — the difference between what was expected and what happened.

**The science:** Schultz (1997, 2007, 2016) established that phasic dopamine neuron activity encodes RPE, not absolute reward. This is now the dominant model in computational neuroscience (Glimcher, 2011, PNAS). For ADHD specifically, Luman et al. and Frank et al. demonstrated that the RPE signal is attenuated — meaning ADHD learners need LARGER prediction errors to get the same dopamine response. The practical implication: predictable rewards lose their effect faster in ADHD than in neurotypical learners. Surprise is the variable that matters.

**How to implement:**

**Surprise reward events (unpredictable, violate expectation):**
- **Random song unlock:** After completing a routine drill, occasionally flash: "NEW SONG UNLOCKED: Wish You Were Here!" This wasn't earned through a specific action — it just appeared. The surprise IS the dopamine.
- **Jackpot XP:** Normal chord earns 25 XP. But 1 in 15 times, the same action earns 250 XP with a special animation. No way to predict when it'll hit.
- **Mystery badge:** "You earned a badge! Tap to reveal." The concealment creates anticipation (dopamine fires during ANTICIPATION of reward, not receipt).
- **Difficulty surprise:** Occasionally insert an easier drill after a hard one. The unexpected ease triggers positive RPE — "that was easier than I thought!" This is as rewarding as making a hard drill unexpectedly pay more.

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- Fixed reward schedules (every 5th action = reward). ADHD learners quickly model the pattern and dopamine habituates.
- Predictable "level complete" celebrations. If the learner knows exactly when the celebration comes, RPE = 0, dopamine = baseline.
- Identical reward animations. Vary the visual, sound, and magnitude randomly.

## 5. DELAYED FEEDBACK WITH DECLARATIVE BRIDGING

**What it is:** When immediate corrective feedback would engage the impaired striatal system, use a brief delay with a declarative "bridge" (verbal/visual label) to shift processing to the intact hippocampal system.

**The science:** Frank et al. (2018, Nature Scientific Reports) demonstrated that ADHD learners' feedback-based learning impairment is **modulated by feedback timing**. Immediate feedback engages the striatal procedural system (impaired in ADHD). Slightly delayed feedback (~2-4 seconds) with a declarative cue shifts processing to the hippocampal declarative system (intact in ADHD). The study showed that ADHD participants' learning improved under delayed feedback conditions that engaged declarative processing.

**How to implement:**

**Chord transition feedback:** Instead of an instant green/red indicator:
1. Learner plays the transition
2. Brief pause (1-2 seconds)
3. Show a labeled visual: "Your ring finger was on fret 2 — it needs fret 3" (declarative, explicit)
4. Replay the correct version (observational)

This gives the hippocampus time to encode the correction declaratively rather than forcing the impaired basal ganglia to do it procedurally.

**After a drill:** Instead of instant scoring, show a brief replay of the learner's performance with annotations. "You nailed the G→C transition 4 out of 5 times. The miss was on beat 3 — your index finger was late."

**Practical constraint:** Don't overdo the delay. More than 3–4 seconds and attention drifts. The sweet spot is 1–3 seconds of pause with a visual bridge.

## 6. ADAPTIVE DIFFICULTY (Staying in the Flow Channel)

**What it is:** Dynamically adjusting task difficulty to match the learner's current performance, keeping them in the "flow zone" — not so easy it's boring, not so hard it's frustrating.

**The science:** Klingberg et al. (2005) demonstrated that adaptive difficulty training (adjusting to user performance in real-time) produced significant improvements in working memory, inhibition control, and complex reasoning in ADHD children — effects that persisted 3 months post-training. Non-adaptive training (fixed difficulty) showed minimal effects. The key mechanism: adaptive difficulty maintains optimal cognitive load, which maximizes both engagement and neural plasticity. For ADHD, where the engagement window is extremely narrow, adaptive difficulty is the difference between "in the zone" and "checked out."

**How to implement:**

**Real-time tempo adaptation:**
- If learner hits 3 clean transitions in a row → BPM increases by 3
- If learner misses 2 transitions in a row → BPM decreases by 5
- The tempo constantly seeks the learner's edge — always slightly challenging, never overwhelming

**Chord complexity adaptation:**
- If a learner breezes through a 2-chord drill → offer a 3-chord drill
- If a learner struggles with a 3-chord drill → drop back to 2 chords
- This happens within a session, not just between sessions

**Song difficulty scaling:**
- Same song, multiple arrangements: Em-only version → Em+Am version → full chord version
- The app detects mastery level and serves the right arrangement

**Progress markers should reflect adaptive difficulty:**
- "You played at 85 BPM today — your fastest ever!" (personal best, not absolute standard)
- Never compare to other learners. Always compare to the learner's own past.

## 7. MULTIMODAL ENCODING (Visual + Auditory + Motor + Verbal Simultaneously)

**What it is:** Engaging multiple sensory channels and cognitive systems simultaneously to create denser, more retrievable memory traces.

**The science:** ADDitude Magazine's synthesis of memory research (Sandra Rief, 2005) and the ADDA's evidence review confirm that ADHD memory is weakest when information enters through a single channel. Multi-modal encoding creates redundant memory pathways — if one pathway fails (as it often does with ADHD working memory), another pathway still holds the information. The more associations, the stickier the memory. Research on Universal Design for Learning (PMC, 2023) showed that multi-modal approaches specifically designed for ADHD produced significantly greater improvements than traditional single-channel instruction.

**How to implement:**

**Every chord introduction should engage 4 channels simultaneously:**

1. **Visual:** See the chord diagram, finger positions lit up on screen
2. **Auditory:** Hear the chord sound — the rich, warm tone of the correct chord
3. **Motor:** Feel the fingers on the strings/keys. Physical sensation.
4. **Verbal:** Say the chord name out loud. "This is G major." (Or hear it named)

**Color-sound association:**
- Each chord has a consistent color across ALL representations (diagram, keyboard display, song sheets, progress grid)
- The color appears every time the chord sounds
- Over time, the learner associates color → sound → shape → name as one integrated concept

**Rhythm + movement:**
- When learning strumming/LH patterns, tap a foot or nod to the beat
- Physical whole-body rhythm recruits motor cortex alongside auditory and visual processing
- Cross-lateral movement (mentioned in ADDA research) improves retention — tapping the opposite foot while strumming engages both hemispheres

**Naming chords out loud:**
- During Chord Flash drills, the learner should SAY the chord name as they play it
- Verbal encoding adds a linguistic memory trace alongside the motor/auditory/visual traces
- For guitar: "Em" (form shape) → strum → hear sound = 3 channels in <2 seconds
- For piano: "C major" (press keys) → hear sound → see lit keys = 3 channels in <2 seconds

## 8. INTERLEAVED PRACTICE WITH CONTEXTUAL INTERFERENCE

**What it is:** Mixing different skills within a single practice block rather than drilling one skill at a time. This feels harder in the moment but produces dramatically better long-term retention.

**The science:** The interleaving effect is one of the most robust findings in motor learning research (Shea & Morgan, 1979; Bjork, 1994). For ADHD learners, it has a dual advantage: (1) the novelty of switching between tasks maintains dopamine-driven engagement, and (2) the "contextual interference" — the difficulty of switching — forces deeper encoding. Spaced repetition research (Donovan & Radosevich, 1999 meta-analysis of 116 studies) confirms that distributed practice outperforms massed practice across all skill types, with the effect being even more pronounced for complex motor skills like instrument playing.

**How to implement:**

**Within each 12-minute session:** The session template already interleaves (Review → New → Song → Victory), but push this further:

- **Review Riff** should include a DIFFERENT chord pair than what was practiced yesterday. Not the most recent — one from 3-5 days ago. This creates retrieval difficulty (desirable difficulty).
- **New Move** segment should include 60 seconds of an older chord interspersed with the new one. Learning Em? Do 30 seconds of Em, then 15 seconds of A (from 5 sessions ago), then 30 seconds of Em, then 15 seconds of D (from 3 sessions ago), then 60 seconds of Em.
- **Song Slice** should occasionally use a song from a PREVIOUS level, not the current one. "Surprise! Play 'Three Little Birds' again — but with your new campfire strum pattern."

**Practice mode rotation rule:** The anti-boredom system should NEVER allow the same practice format in consecutive segments, AND should interleave formats from different skill categories:
- Chord recognition (visual) → Transition drill (motor) → Song play (integrated) → Ear training (auditory)
- NOT: Chord recognition → Chord recognition → Chord recognition

**The 3-item interleave minimum:** Never practice fewer than 3 different chords/skills in a single session. Even if the learner is "focusing" on a new chord, the session must include at least 2 review items.

## 9. SOCIAL ACCOUNTABILITY WITHOUT COMPETITION

**What it is:** Creating gentle social connection around practice without leaderboards, rankings, or competitive elements that trigger ADHD shame/rejection sensitivity.

**The science:** "Body doubling" — working in the presence of another person — is one of the most consistently effective ADHD focus strategies (Queens Online School, 2025). The mechanism: external social presence provides enough ambient accountability to bridge the initiation gap without triggering performance anxiety. Competitive elements (leaderboards, rankings) are counterproductive for ADHD because rejection sensitivity dysphoria (RSD) means any negative comparison triggers disproportionate emotional pain, leading to app abandonment. The optimal social element is **shared presence without shared judgment**.

**How to implement:**

**Practice Buddy (async):**
- Learner can invite one person to be their "practice buddy"
- Buddy receives a gentle notification: "Scott practiced for 12 minutes today! 🎸"
- Buddy can send a 👍 or a short text reply
- NO data about quality, speed, or accuracy is shared — only that practice happened
- This provides just enough external accountability to trigger the initiation impulse

**Community challenges (cooperative, not competitive):**
- "This week, ChordSpark learners collectively practiced for 50,000 minutes. Can we hit 60,000?"
- The learner contributes to a group goal — their individual performance is invisible
- Progress bar shows the community's aggregate progress
- Reward unlocks for everyone when the community goal is met

**Shared milestones (not rankings):**
- "You're the 10,000th person to learn the G chord!"
- "43% of ChordSpark learners are working on Level 4 this week — just like you"
- These create belonging without hierarchy

**Anti-patterns:**
- NO leaderboards. Ever. ADHD + leaderboards = shame spiral.
- NO "John practiced 45 minutes today!" comparisons
- NO streaks displayed publicly
- NO achievement rarity ("only 2% of users have this badge!" = pressure, not motivation)

## Integration Summary: Where Each Technique Lives

| Technique | Primary Location | Effect |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|
| 1. Stretching the Ratios | XP/reward system, all sessions | Builds extinction-resistant practice habits |
| 2. Implementation Intentions | Onboarding, session start, notifications | 2-3x follow-through on practice commitment |
| 3. Observational Before Feedback | Every New Move segment | Bypasses impaired procedural system |
| 4. Prediction Error Optimization | Reward timing/magnitude randomization | Maintains dopamine engagement long-term |
| 5. Delayed Feedback + Declarative Bridge | Transition feedback, drill scoring | Shifts learning to intact hippocampal system |
| 6. Adaptive Difficulty | Tempo, chord count, song arrangement | Keeps learner in flow zone (narrow for ADHD) |
| 7. Multimodal Encoding | Chord introductions, all practice modes | Creates redundant memory traces |
| 8. Interleaved Practice | Session structure, review scheduling | Deeper encoding + novelty maintenance |
| 9. Social Accountability | Practice buddy, community challenges | Bridges initiation gap without shame |
